# Notes: Language Assessment

## Key Sources

- ALTE Manual for Language Test Development and Examining, 2011.
- EALTA Guidelines for Good Practice.
- Cambridge English Principles of Good Practice, 2016.
- Wind & Peterson, 2018, systematic review of rating quality.
- Fan & Yan, 2020, narrative review of speaking assessment.
- Jang & Sawaki, 2025, AI in language assessment editorial.
- O'Grady et al., 2026, AI in language assessment scoping review.

## What Seems Solid

- Good assessment starts with purpose: what decision is being made, for whom, and with what consequences.
- Validity is about the interpretation and use of scores, not just item quality.
- Reliability matters especially for rater-mediated tasks such as writing and speaking.
- Fairness includes accessibility, bias reduction, transparent criteria, and avoiding construct-irrelevant barriers.
- Practicality matters: longer tests can collect more evidence but may cause fatigue and reduce feasibility.
- AI scoring can be useful for feedback and low-stakes diagnostics, but needs transparency, monitoring, and human validation before high-stakes use.

## Writing Assessment Notes

- Analytic rubrics are useful because they separate task achievement, organization, language control, vocabulary, and register.
- Rating quality depends on clear criteria, benchmark samples, rater calibration, and monitoring of consistency.
- For LULT, calibration examples should be treated as anchors, not as exhaustive score rules.

## Speaking Assessment Notes

- Speaking is dynamic and context-dependent: task type, interaction format, planning time, interlocutor, and topic all affect performance.
- Without audio, pronunciation, fluency, pausing, stress, intonation, and real-time repair cannot be assessed directly.
- A text-only “speaking proxy” should be labeled as discourse/planning evidence, not true speaking evidence.

## AI-Based Assessment Notes

- LLM scores can be unstable across prompts, model versions, and response formats.
- LLMs may overvalue surface features such as lexical sophistication and under-detect communicative adequacy or memorized text.
- LULT should avoid high-stakes claims, keep confidence labels, and invite human review for consequential decisions.

## Practical Implications For LULT

- Final report should include observed evidence, missing evidence, and confidence.
- The GPT should use multiple short tasks plus at least one open production task.
- The GPT should separate diagnostic score from motivation language.
- Regression prompts and benchmark examples are part of quality monitoring.
